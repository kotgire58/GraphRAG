# Treatment Protocols — Standard of Care Guidelines

## Protocol: Type 2 Diabetes First-Line Management
- Protocol ID: DIAB-001
- Issuing body: American Diabetes Association
- Version: 2024 Standards of Care
- First-line pharmacotherapy: Metformin (unless contraindicated)
- Metformin initiation criteria: GFR at or above 45 mL/min/1.73m2
- Metformin dose reduction threshold: GFR 30 to 44 mL/min/1.73m2 (reduce dose, increase monitoring)
- Metformin absolute contraindication threshold: GFR below 30 mL/min/1.73m2
- Second-line options: GLP-1 receptor agonists (preferred with cardiovascular disease), SGLT2 inhibitors (preferred with heart failure or CKD), sulfonylureas (lowest cost)
- HbA1c monitoring frequency: Every 3 months until stable, then every 6 months
- Blood pressure target in diabetic patients: Below 130/80 mmHg
- Preferred antihypertensive in diabetic patients with proteinuria: ACE inhibitor or ARB
- Reason for ACE inhibitor preference: Reduces proteinuria and slows diabetic nephropathy progression independent of blood pressure effect
- Renal monitoring: GFR and urine albumin every year at minimum

## Protocol: Hypertension Management in Diabetic Patients
- Protocol ID: HTN-DM-001
- Issuing body: Joint National Committee / American Heart Association
- Blood pressure target: Below 130/80 mmHg
- First-line agent for diabetic patients with CKD or proteinuria: ACE inhibitor (Lisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril) or ARB (Losartan, Valsartan)
- First-line agent for diabetic patients without CKD: ACE inhibitor, ARB, calcium channel blocker, or thiazide diuretic
- Combination therapy: Often required; ACE inhibitor plus calcium channel blocker preferred second combination
- Avoid combination: ACE inhibitor plus ARB (dual RAAS blockade increases hyperkalemia and AKI risk without additional benefit)
- Avoid combination: ACE inhibitor plus potassium-sparing diuretic in patients with CKD (hyperkalemia risk)
- Electrolyte monitoring: Serum potassium and creatinine 1 to 2 weeks after starting ACE inhibitor, then periodically
- Potassium threshold for ACE inhibitor caution: Baseline serum potassium above 5.0 mEq/L

## Protocol: Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation
- Protocol ID: AFIB-ANTICOAG-001
- Issuing body: American Heart Association / American College of Cardiology
- Risk assessment tool: CHA2DS2-VASc score
- Anticoagulation recommendation threshold: CHA2DS2-VASc score 2 or above in men, 3 or above in women
- Warfarin target INR: 2.0 to 3.0
- Warfarin INR monitoring: Weekly when initiating or adjusting, monthly when stable
- INR above 4.0 action: Hold dose, investigate cause, recheck INR within 24 to 48 hours
- INR above 10.0 action: Consider vitamin K administration, urgent assessment
- Factors that increase INR on warfarin: CYP2C9 inhibitors, antibiotics reducing gut flora, dietary vitamin K reduction, liver disease, heart failure
- Factors that decrease INR on warfarin: CYP2C9 inducers, dietary vitamin K increase
- Monitoring escalation trigger: Any new medication addition requires INR recheck within 1 week

## Protocol: Immunosuppression Management Post-Transplant
- Protocol ID: TRANSPLANT-001
- Issuing body: American Society of Transplantation
- Standard triple therapy: Calcineurin inhibitor (Tacrolimus or Cyclosporine) plus Mycophenolate plus Corticosteroid
- Tacrolimus target trough (first year): 8 to 12 ng/mL
- Tacrolimus target trough (after first year): 5 to 8 ng/mL
- Tacrolimus monitoring frequency: Daily when initiating; weekly when stable; more frequently when adding new medications
- Trigger for immediate Tacrolimus level check: Any new drug with CYP3A4 inhibitory or inductive potential
- Tacrolimus toxicity signs: Nephrotoxicity (rising creatinine), neurotoxicity (tremor, headache, seizures), hypertension, hyperglycemia
- Cyclosporine target trough: 100 to 200 ng/mL (varies by transplant type and time post-transplant)
- Antifungal prophylaxis: Fluconazole commonly used for first 1 to 3 months post-transplant
- Antifungal prophylaxis dose requiring immunosuppressant dose adjustment: Any therapeutic dose of fluconazole

## Protocol: Antifungal Prescribing for Candidiasis
- Protocol ID: ANTIFUNG-001
- Issuing body: Infectious Diseases Society of America
- Oropharyngeal candidiasis first-line: Fluconazole 100 to 200mg daily for 7 to 14 days
- Vaginal candidiasis uncomplicated: Fluconazole 150mg single dose
- Vaginal candidiasis complicated (recurrent): Fluconazole 150mg every 72 hours for 3 doses, then weekly for 6 months
- Esophageal candidiasis: Fluconazole 200 to 400mg daily for 14 to 21 days
- Candidemia: Fluconazole 800mg loading dose then 400mg daily (if not critically ill and susceptible strain)
- Pre-prescribing check required: Review all current medications for CYP2C9 interactions
- Mandatory review before prescribing fluconazole: Warfarin (bleeding risk), sulfonylureas (hypoglycemia risk), phenytoin (toxicity risk), tacrolimus (nephrotoxicity risk), cyclosporine (nephrotoxicity risk)
- Dose adjustment note for interactions: Dose reduction of interacting drug or enhanced monitoring required

## Protocol: Statin Prescribing and Monitoring
- Protocol ID: STATIN-001
- Issuing body: American College of Cardiology / American Heart Association
- High-intensity statin indication: LDL above 190 mg/dL, established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, diabetes with additional risk factors
- CYP3A4 interaction note for simvastatin: Maximum dose 10mg when combined with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors; 20mg with moderate inhibitors
- Simvastatin absolute contraindication: Combined use with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole, itraconazole, clarithromycin, cyclosporine)
- Atorvastatin note: Also CYP3A4 substrate but lower interaction risk than simvastatin due to different metabolism extent
- Rhabdomyolysis monitoring: CK levels if patient reports muscle pain, weakness, or brown urine
- Rhabdomyolysis risk factors: High statin dose, CYP3A4 inhibitor co-administration, hypothyroidism, renal impairment

## Protocol: Digoxin Use in Heart Failure and Atrial Fibrillation
- Protocol ID: DIGOXIN-001
- Issuing body: American Heart Association
- Target serum digoxin level: 0.5 to 0.9 ng/mL (lower end preferred for heart failure benefit)
- Toxic level: Above 2.0 ng/mL
- Monitoring frequency: Baseline, 1 week after initiation, then every 6 months when stable; immediately if toxicity suspected
- Dose reduction required: Renal impairment (GFR below 60), advanced age, low body weight
- Factors increasing digoxin toxicity risk: Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypercalcemia, hypothyroidism, renal impairment
- P-glycoprotein interaction note: P-gp inhibitors (clarithromycin, amiodarone, verapamil, quinidine) significantly increase digoxin levels
- Action required with P-gp inhibitor addition: Reduce digoxin dose by 30 to 50 percent; recheck level in 1 week

## Protocol: Metformin Safety Monitoring
- Protocol ID: METFORM-SAFETY-001
- Issuing body: FDA and American Diabetes Association
- Renal function monitoring: At baseline, then at least annually; every 3 to 6 months if GFR 45 to 60
- Hold Metformin conditions: GFR below 30; iodinated contrast procedure (hold 48 hours before and after); surgery requiring general anesthesia; dehydration; acute illness with risk of dehydration
- Contrast procedure protocol: Hold Metformin the day of and 48 hours after IV contrast; resume only after confirming renal function stable
- Lactic acidosis warning signs: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, hyperventilation, drowsiness
- Drug interactions affecting Metformin clearance: OCT2 inhibitors (cimetidine, trimethoprim, vandetanib); MATE inhibitors
- Alcohol warning: Excessive alcohol potentiates lactic acidosis risk
- Liver disease contraindication: Active liver disease or ALT above 3 times upper limit of normal
