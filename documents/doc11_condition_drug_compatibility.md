# Condition-Drug Compatibility and Dosing Adjustments

## Renal Impairment Drug Dosing

### GFR 60 to 89 (Mild CKD)
- Metformin: Continue at standard dose; monitor renal function every 6 months
- Lisinopril: Continue at standard dose; monitor potassium and creatinine
- Fluconazole: No dose adjustment required
- Warfarin: No dose adjustment; monitor INR as usual
- Digoxin: Reduce dose by 25 percent; monitor levels
- Tacrolimus: No initial adjustment; monitor levels
- Atorvastatin: No dose adjustment
- Simvastatin: No dose adjustment

### GFR 30 to 59 (Moderate CKD)
- Metformin: Reduce to maximum 1000mg daily; monitor renal function every 3 months; hold if acute illness
- Lisinopril: Use with caution; start at low dose; monitor potassium closely (CKD increases hyperkalemia risk with ACE inhibitors)
- Fluconazole: No dose adjustment for single doses; for multiple days, monitor renal function
- Warfarin: No dose adjustment; INR monitoring unchanged but acute kidney injury can unpredictably alter INR
- Digoxin: Reduce dose by 25 to 50 percent; monitor levels every 3 months
- Tacrolimus: Monitor levels more frequently; nephrotoxicity worsens existing CKD
- Atorvastatin: No dose adjustment
- Simvastatin: No dose adjustment but rhabdomyolysis worsens outcome in CKD

### GFR 15 to 29 (Severe CKD)
- Metformin: Contraindicated; stop immediately; risk of lactic acidosis
- Lisinopril: Use with extreme caution; significant hyperkalemia risk; may need to switch to loop diuretic
- Fluconazole: Reduce dose by 50 percent; use half of standard dose
- Warfarin: Use with caution; uremia can independently prolong PT/INR; more frequent monitoring
- Digoxin: Reduce dose by 50 percent; monitor levels monthly
- Tacrolimus: Existing nephrotoxicity risk amplified; consider switching to non-nephrotoxic immunosuppressant
- Atorvastatin: Start at lowest dose; myopathy risk increased in severe CKD

### GFR below 15 (Kidney Failure / Dialysis)
- Metformin: Absolutely contraindicated
- Lisinopril: Generally avoid; severe hyperkalemia risk; specialist management required
- Fluconazole: 50 percent of standard dose every 48 hours; supplemental dose after hemodialysis
- Warfarin: Continue with close INR monitoring; dialysis patients have complex coagulation abnormalities
- Digoxin: Avoid if possible; extremely narrow therapeutic window; dialysis does not effectively remove digoxin
- Tacrolimus: Continue with level monitoring; transplant specialist management required

## Hepatic Impairment Drug Dosing

### Mild Hepatic Impairment (Child-Pugh A)
- Metformin: Avoid; lactic acidosis risk increased even in mild hepatic impairment
- Warfarin: Reduce dose; liver produces clotting factors; even mild impairment increases sensitivity
- Fluconazole: Use with caution; monitor liver enzymes
- Atorvastatin: Use with caution; start at lowest dose
- Tacrolimus: Monitor levels closely; may need dose reduction

### Moderate to Severe Hepatic Impairment (Child-Pugh B and C)
- Metformin: Contraindicated
- Warfarin: Significant dose reduction required; clotting factor production impaired; INR unpredictable
- Fluconazole: Contraindicated for prolonged courses; serious hepatotoxicity risk
- Atorvastatin: Contraindicated
- Simvastatin: Contraindicated
- Tacrolimus: Dose reduction required; extended half-life; monitor very closely

## Condition-Specific Drug Contraindications

### Type 2 Diabetes — Drugs to Avoid or Use Cautiously
- Corticosteroids (Prednisone, Dexamethasone): Cause significant hyperglycemia; dose increase of antidiabetic drugs often required
- Thiazide diuretics (Hydrochlorothiazide): Worsen glucose control; reduce insulin secretion
- Beta-blockers: Mask hypoglycemia symptoms (tachycardia, tremor) in insulin-treated patients; use with caution
- Atypical antipsychotics (Olanzapine, Clozapine): Significant weight gain and insulin resistance
- Fluoroquinolone antibiotics (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin): May cause hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia unpredictably

### Hypertension — Drugs that Worsen Blood Pressure
- NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen): Cause sodium retention; raise blood pressure; reduce efficacy of antihypertensives
- Decongestants (Pseudoephedrine, Phenylephrine): Vasoconstriction raises blood pressure
- Combined oral contraceptives: Estrogen component raises blood pressure
- Stimulants (Amphetamines, Modafinil): Raise blood pressure
- Cyclosporine and Tacrolimus: Both cause hypertension; common post-transplant complication

### Atrial Fibrillation — QT Prolonging Drugs (Increase Arrhythmia Risk)
- Amiodarone: QT prolongation (also antiarrhythmic)
- Azithromycin: Moderate QT risk; use with caution in existing AF
- Clarithromycin: QT prolongation; prefer azithromycin
- Fluoroquinolones: QT risk
- Fluconazole: Mild to moderate QT prolongation; significant when combined with other QT-prolonging drugs
- Haloperidol: Significant QT prolongation
- Methadone: Significant QT prolongation
- Ondansetron high dose: QT prolongation; use standard doses only

### Heart Failure — Drugs to Avoid
- NSAIDs: Cause sodium and fluid retention; worsen heart failure; can precipitate acute decompensation
- Thiazolidinediones (Pioglitazone, Rosiglitazone): Cause fluid retention; contraindicated in NYHA Class III and IV
- Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (Verapamil, Diltiazem): Negative inotropic effect; worsen HFrEF
- Most antiarrhythmics except amiodarone and dofetilide: Proarrhythmic effects worsen outcomes
- Cilostazol: Contraindicated in heart failure
- Dronedarone: Contraindicated in recent decompensated heart failure

### Post-Transplant — Drugs Requiring Immunosuppressant Adjustment
- Any CYP3A4 inhibitor with Tacrolimus or Cyclosporine: Level increase; dose reduction needed
  - Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors: Ketoconazole, Itraconazole, Voriconazole (increases tacrolimus 3 to 5 fold), Clarithromycin, Erythromycin
  - Moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors: Fluconazole (increases tacrolimus 2 to 4 fold), Diltiazem, Verapamil
- Any CYP3A4 inducer with Tacrolimus or Cyclosporine: Level decrease; dose increase needed
  - Strong CYP3A4 inducers: Rifampin (requires 3 to 5 fold dose increase), Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, St. John's Wort
- NSAID + Calcineurin inhibitor: Additive nephrotoxicity; avoid NSAIDs in all transplant patients
- Live vaccines: Contraindicated in immunosuppressed transplant patients

## Electrolyte Considerations

### Hyperkalemia Risk Combinations
- ACE inhibitor alone in CKD: Moderate risk
- ARB alone in CKD: Moderate risk
- ACE inhibitor + ARB: High risk (do not combine)
- ACE inhibitor + Spironolactone: High risk in CKD; acceptable in heart failure with monitoring
- ACE inhibitor + Spironolactone + CKD (GFR below 45): Very high risk; generally avoid
- ACE inhibitor + NSAID: Moderate risk
- Trimethoprim (antibiotic): Blocks potassium excretion; behaves like potassium-sparing diuretic; significant risk in CKD
- Tacrolimus + Cyclosporine: Both cause hyperkalemia; do not combine

### Hypokalemia Risk (Important because hypokalemia worsens digoxin toxicity)
- Loop diuretics (Furosemide, Bumetanide): Significant potassium loss
- Thiazide diuretics: Moderate potassium loss
- Corticosteroids: Potassium loss
- Amphotericin B: Potassium loss
- Cisplatin: Potassium loss
- Combined loop diuretic + Digoxin: Diuretic causes hypokalemia; hypokalemia increases digoxin toxicity; monitor potassium closely
